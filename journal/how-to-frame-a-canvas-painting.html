How to Frame a Canvas Painting

A frame should give the work an edge, not a disguise. The right choice protects the structure, respects the surface and lets the painting keep its own atmosphere.

A stretched canvas does not automatically need a decorative frame. Some paintings are complete with their painted or finished edges exposed; others gain presence from a narrow floating frame or the visual weight of a traditional moulding. The decision begins with the object itself — its support, depth, condition and surface — before it reaches the colour of the room.

This guide is for paintings already stretched over wooden bars. If the canvas is rolled, loose, unusually large, distorted or carrying fragile texture, ask an experienced art framer or conservator to inspect it before altering anything.

First, identify what you actually own

Look at the back and edges without touching the painted surface. A stretched canvas is tensioned around a wooden stretcher and usually stapled or tacked at the reverse. A canvas panel is fabric adhered to a rigid board. A work on paper that resembles canvas may need an entirely different mounting and glazing system.

Record the artist, title, medium, overall dimensions and any care notes from the certificate of authenticity. Mixed media, metallic leaf, collage and projecting elements may require extra clearance. When the medium is uncertain, do not assume that a standard ready-made frame is safe.

Choose among three presentations

Unframed canvas

A gallery-wrapped canvas can hang without an outer frame when the fabric is neatly secured at the back, the edges are intentionally finished and the stretcher is deep enough to read as a complete object. This is the lightest and most minimal option. It also leaves corners and sides exposed to knocks, so it is less forgiving in a hallway or busy room.

Floating frame

A canvas float frame surrounds the painting with a small visible gap, often called a reveal. The canvas appears to hover inside the moulding, and its full face remains visible. This suits contemporary paintings and textured edges because nothing overlaps the image. The reveal should be even on every side, and the frame must be deep enough to contain the stretcher without projecting awkwardly beyond it.

Traditional frame

A traditional frame overlaps the front edge slightly with a lip. It can lend architectural weight, connect a painting with older furniture or visually contain a restless composition. Confirm that the lip does not cover a signature or essential painted detail. A framer may use a liner or spacer to keep ornate moulding from competing with a highly textured surface.

The frame is the painting's final pause: enough separation from the wall for the image to begin, never so much decoration that the work has to fight its way out.

Does a canvas painting need glass?

Most stable oil and acrylic paintings on stretched canvas are displayed without glass. Glazing can introduce reflections, add considerable weight and create a confined space in front of a surface designed to be seen directly. Glass must never press against paint or raised texture.

Glazing may be appropriate for fragile, friable or unusually sensitive media, but that is a conservation decision rather than a decorative default. Works on paper and many fine art prints generally do benefit from protective glazing, archival materials and a spacer or mat that keeps the image away from the glass. If a canvas includes charcoal, pastel, loose pigment or delicate collage, ask a conservator or specialist framer for a system designed around the exact materials.

Measure the canvas, not the hoped-for frame

Measure the artwork in three dimensions: width, height and stretcher depth. Take each face measurement in more than one place because handmade stretchers are not always perfectly square. Use the largest reading and tell the framer about any protruding keys, staples or hardware on the back.

The finished outer dimensions depend on moulding width and, for a float frame, the reveal. Ask the framer to state the final size before approving the order. That number matters when the painting must fit between furniture, trim, switches or another artwork. If you are still selecting the work, compare these measurements with the guide to choosing original art for your home.

Let the artwork lead the finish

Begin with the painting's dominant visual character, not a perfect match to the sofa. A slim black or dark wood frame can create a clean boundary; natural wood can soften a cool interior; a restrained metallic finish can answer small passages of gold without turning them into a theme. White frames tend to merge with pale walls, while high contrast makes the outer edge more emphatic.

View corner samples beside the actual painting in the room's daytime and evening light. A phone image can flatten colour and scale, so do not make a costly framing decision from a screen alone when an in-person comparison is possible. For a complex work, two quiet samples are often more revealing than twenty dramatic ones.

Protect the original structure

A frame should support the canvas without adhesive touching the artwork and without screws driven through the face of the stretcher. Reversible mechanical fittings allow the work to be removed without damage. Do not trim a loose canvas, paint its edges to suit a frame or glue it to a board simply to make it fit.

Keep labels, inscriptions and the back of the canvas visible or carefully documented. Ask the framer to retain any original hardware that is removed and to avoid covering identifying information. These details belong to the work's physical history.

Plan hardware and placement together

The frame adds weight, sometimes substantially. Use hanging hardware rated for the complete framed work and suitable for the wall construction. Large, glazed or valuable pieces are best installed by a professional. In earthquake-prone Los Angeles, locking or seismic hardware can reduce the risk of a work lifting from its supports.

Before drilling, follow the measurements in the guide to how high to hang art. Leave enough space for the outer frame, check the path of direct sunlight and avoid heat, moisture and air vents. The article on caring for original art explains why a stable position is more protective than frequent cleaning.

A framing checklist for collectors

  • Confirm whether the work is stretched canvas, panel, paper or mixed media.
  • Measure width, height and depth at several points.
  • Choose unframed, floating or traditional presentation before choosing colour.
  • Ask whether the image edge, signature and textured elements will remain clear.
  • Confirm the final outside dimensions, total weight and included hanging hardware.
  • Request reversible fitting and conservation-appropriate materials.
  • Keep receipts, specifications and photographs with the artwork's records.

When considering an available MARIEZAL original, request exact dimensions, edge photographs and medium details before ordering a frame. For presentation questions about a particular work, contact the studio. A good frame does not make the painting more authentic or more important. It simply creates the conditions in which the work can remain fully itself.
